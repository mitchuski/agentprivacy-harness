# Home Prep for a 72-Hour Power Outage

Prepare an ordinary household to manage safely and comfortably through an outage of up to 72 hours; assumes a temperate climate and no backup generator. Work the sections in order; earlier ones matter most.

## Water
Store 4 litres per person per day (12 litres each for 72 hours): half for drinking, half for cooking and basic hygiene. Use food-grade containers kept out of direct sunlight; replace stored water every 6 months. If a storm is forecast, also fill the bathtub: that water is for flushing toilets and washing, never drinking.

## Food and cold storage
Keep a 3-day shelf of non-perishable food needing no cooking: canned beans, canned fish, peanut butter, crackers, dried fruit, shelf-stable milk. Own a manual can opener; an electric one is useless. Eat perishables from the refrigerator first, then the freezer, then the emergency shelf. An unopened refrigerator stays safe about 4 hours; a full freezer holds safe temperature about 48 hours, a half-full one only about 24, so keep the freezer consolidated and full, even with containers of frozen water. Keep both doors closed as much as possible; tape a reminder note to each. Discard any perishable over 2 hours above 4°C; when in doubt, throw it out.

## Light
Battery-powered LED lanterns or headlamps are your primary light; avoid candles entirely, a leading cause of house fires during outages. Store at least one light per person plus two spare battery sets each. Keep a headlamp beside each bed and a lantern in the kitchen, findable in the dark.

## Heat and safety
Never run a camping stove, barbecue, or generator indoors, in a garage, or near a window: each produces carbon monoxide, odourless and potentially fatal. Install at least one battery-powered carbon-monoxide alarm on each floor where people sleep. In cold weather, gather everyone in one small room on the home's south-facing side with its door closed; layered clothing and blankets beat any improvised heater. If indoor temperature falls below 10°C and you cannot stay warm, relocate to a warming centre or a neighbour's home.

## Communication
Keep a battery-powered or hand-crank radio for emergency broadcasts and learn your local emergency station's frequency beforehand. Charge every phone and power bank whenever a storm is forecast; a full 20,000 mAh power bank recharges a typical phone about 4 times. Switch phones to low-power mode when the outage starts. Agree in advance on one out-of-area contact everyone will text; texts beat voice calls on congested networks.

## Medical
Keep a first-aid kit and a 7-day supply of every prescription medication, rotated to stay in date. Refrigerated medication such as insulin generally stays usable at room temperature up to 28 days; confirm your medication's rule with a pharmacist beforehand. Write pharmacy, doctor, and poison-control numbers on paper: a dead phone is not a phone book.

## When power returns
Wait 5 minutes before switching major appliances back on, letting the grid stabilise. Freezer food still holding ice crystals can be safely refrozen. Restock everything used within one week, water and batteries first. Write a one-page note on what was missing or awkward, and fix at least one gap before the next outage.
